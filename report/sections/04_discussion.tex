\subsection{XGBoost and LightGBM: Performance and Efficiency}
\label{sec:gbdt-discussion}

Across all five benchmark datasets, XGBoost and LightGBM consistently deliver competitive or
superior performance compared to the other baselines, while remaining among the fastest models
to train.

\paragraph{Regression tasks.}
On the Diamonds dataset, both GBDTs substantially outperform xRFM, achieving test RMSEs of
544.7 (XGBoost) and 536.0 (LightGBM) against xRFM's 1{,}496.7 — a reduction in error of
approximately 64\%. This gap is consistent with the axis-aligned inductive bias of tree-based
models: diamond price is largely determined by a small set of monotone features (carat, physical
dimensions) that GBDT splits can exploit directly~\citep{grinsztajn2022tree}. On Superconductivity,
the advantage is much smaller — LightGBM achieves RMSE 9.21 versus xRFM's 9.45 (a 2.5\%
improvement), indicating that the high-dimensional, less axis-aligned structure of this dataset
partially closes the gap.

\paragraph{Classification tasks.}
On the Online Shoppers dataset, both GBDTs improve on xRFM in AUC (0.931 vs.\ 0.902), reflecting
their ability to capture non-linear, feature-interaction effects from the larger training set
($n = 7{,}398$). On the Stroke dataset, XGBoost achieves the highest AUC of 0.839, markedly
above xRFM's 0.761; the MLP result (AUC $= 0.269$) appears anomalous and is consistent with
class-imbalance sensitivity in the absence of explicit resampling. Notably, on the HR Attrition
dataset — the smallest dataset with the highest feature dimensionality ($n = 882$, $d = 51$)
— xRFM marginally outperforms XGBoost in accuracy (0.867 vs.\ 0.857), suggesting that kernel
feature learning provides a modest advantage in the high-$d$, low-$n$ regime.

\paragraph{Scalability.}
The scalability experiments (Table~\ref{tab:scaling}) reveal that LightGBM is the fastest model
across all sample sizes. At $n = 30{,}000$ on the Diamonds dataset, LightGBM trains in 0.29 s
compared to 0.59 s for XGBoost and 22.2 s for MLP — a speed advantage exceeding 75$\times$
over MLP at this scale. Both GBDTs show near-linear growth in training time with sample size,
consistent with their histogram-based split-finding algorithms. In terms of predictive performance,
RMSE decreases sub-linearly with $n$: moving from 1{,}000 to 5{,}000 samples yields a larger
improvement than moving from 10{,}000 to 30{,}000, indicating diminishing returns at larger sizes.

In summary, XGBoost and LightGBM represent a strong, efficient baseline for tabular data: they
are robust across dataset sizes and task types, require minimal preprocessing, and train
significantly faster than MLP at large scale. Their primary limitation relative to xRFM appears
only in small, high-dimensional classification settings where kernel feature learning can better
capture the global feature geometry.
