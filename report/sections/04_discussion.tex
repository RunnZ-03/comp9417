\subsection{XGBoost and LightGBM: Performance and Efficiency}
\label{sec:gbdt-discussion}

XGBoost and LightGBM deliver competitive or superior performance across all five datasets while
remaining the fastest models to train. On Diamonds, both GBDTs outperform xRFM by $\sim$64\%
in RMSE (544.7 / 536.0 vs.\ 1{,}496.7), consistent with the axis-aligned inductive bias of
tree-based splits~\citep{grinsztajn2022tree}. On HR Attrition ($n=882$, $d=51$), xRFM
marginally surpasses XGBoost in accuracy (0.867 vs.\ 0.857), suggesting kernel feature learning
offers a modest advantage only in the high-$d$, low-$n$ regime. LightGBM is the fastest model
at every scale — 75$\times$ faster than MLP at $n=30{,}000$ — with near-linear growth in
training time (Table~\ref{tab:scaling}).


% ── 4.3 MLP Analysis (D负责) ─────────────────────────────────────────────────
\subsection{MLP: Performance and Scalability Limitations}
\label{sec:mlp-discussion}

On Diamonds, MLP underperforms both XGBoost and LightGBM by a large margin in RMSE 
(743.5 vs.\ 542.7 / 535.3 at $n=30{,}000$), and a similar pattern is observed on 
Superconductivity (11.22 vs.\ $\sim$9.5 RMSE) (Table~\ref{tab:main-results}), indicating weaker ability to exploit 
feature interactions, especially in high-dimensional settings~\citep{tolstikhin2021mlp}. In classification tasks, 
performance is unstable: while accuracy can be comparable in some cases (e.g., Stroke, 0.9511), 
probability calibration degrades significantly, as reflected by the low AUC (0.269) (Table~\ref{tab:main-results}), 
highlighting sensitivity to class imbalance~\citep{walde2004statistical}. Unlike tree-based methods such as XGBoost and LightGBM, 
MLPs rely on global gradient-based optimisation over continuous parameter spaces. 
This makes them less aligned with the piecewise and often sparse structure of tabular features, 
resulting in lower data efficiency.
