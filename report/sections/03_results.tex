\section{Results}

This section compares xRFM with XGBoost, LightGBM, and MLP in terms of predictive performance, computational efficiency, scalability, and interpretability. All models are evaluated using the same preprocessing pipeline, train-validation-test split, and evaluation metrics described in Section~2.

\paragraph{Regression tasks.}
LightGBM achieves the lowest RMSE on both regression datasets. xRFM is competitive on Superconductivity, where its RMSE of 9.454 is close to LightGBM's 9.209 and XGBoost's 9.302. However, xRFM performs much worse on Diamonds, with RMSE 1496.72 compared with 535.99 for LightGBM and 544.68 for XGBoost. This suggests that AGOP-based feature learning does not automatically outperform tree ensembles on large mixed-type tabular regression.

\paragraph{Classification tasks.}
For Online Shoppers and Stroke, XGBoost and LightGBM obtain the strongest AUC-ROC scores. xRFM remains reasonably competitive on Online Shoppers, but its AUC is below the boosted-tree models. xRFM's strongest result is on HR Attrition, where it achieves the highest accuracy of 0.8673, suggesting a modest advantage in small-sample classification. MLP obtains high accuracy on Stroke but very low AUC, showing that accuracy alone can be misleading under severe class imbalance.

\subsection{Scalability Analysis}

Scalability experiments (detailed in Table~\ref{tab:scaling-full}, Appendix~\ref{app:tables}) reveal a significant efficiency gap between models. As visualised in Figure~\ref{fig:scaling}, xRFM exhibits near-quadratic time complexity on the Diamonds dataset, where training time increases exponentially with marginal RMSE gains (reaching 226.44s at $n=30{,}000$). In contrast, GBDT baselines maintain sub-second training times even at full scale. 

However, xRFM scales more effectively on the purely numerical Superconductivity dataset, nearly matching GBDT performance. This divergence confirms that xRFM's AGOP-based reweighting is better suited for continuous high-dimensional inputs than for mixed-type data with sharp categorical thresholds.

\subsection{Interpretability Comparison}

AGOP-diagonal rankings (detailed in Table~\ref{tab:xrfm-interpretability-full}, Appendix~\ref{app:tables}) align closely with domain knowledge across all tasks, identifying physical dimensions for Diamonds and health risk factors like glucose and age for Stroke. 

To answer whether AGOP agrees with other feature-importance methods, we perform a detailed comparison on the Diamonds dataset (Table~\ref{tab:diamonds-feature-importance} and Figure~\ref{fig:diamonds-feature-importance-comparison}).

The empirical comparison partly supports the theoretical argument made in Section~2.1. AGOP successfully identifies continuous features (\texttt{y}, \texttt{x}, \texttt{carat}) that the learned xRFM function is highly sensitive to, agreeing with mutual information. However, permutation importance assigns additional weight to categorical quality features (e.g., \texttt{color\_I}) that AGOP downplays. 

This suggests that while AGOP identifies sensible predictive directions, its rankings do not guarantee overall accuracy. On Diamonds, despite identifying relevant size features, xRFM's RMSE remains nearly three times higher than tree-based models. Interpretability and predictive power are decoupled here: AGOP highlights the sensitivity of the fitted model, but the xRFM pipeline itself struggles to capture sharp axis-aligned thresholds and categorical interactions as effectively as boosted trees.
