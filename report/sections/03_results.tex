\section{Results}

\subsection{Predictive Performance}

Table~\ref{tab:main-results} reports held-out test performance across all five datasets.
LightGBM achieves the lowest RMSE on both regression tasks; xRFM is competitive on Superconductivity
(9.454 vs.\ 9.209) but nearly three times worse on Diamonds (1496.72 vs.\ 535.99), where strong
categorical and threshold effects favour boosted-tree splits.
For classification, XGBoost and LightGBM lead on AUC-ROC; xRFM achieves the highest accuracy on
HR Attrition (0.8673), while MLP yields anomalously low AUC on Stroke (0.269) under severe class imbalance.

\subsection{Scalability Analysis}

Table~\ref{tab:scaling} and Figure~\ref{fig:scaling} report test RMSE and training time versus
$n_\text{train}$ on Diamonds and Superconductivity.
xRFM training time grows from 0.77\,s to 226.44\,s as $n_\text{train}$ increases from 1{,}000 to
30{,}000, while RMSE barely improves (1657 to 1488); LightGBM trains in under one second at every
scale and reaches RMSE 535. On Superconductivity, xRFM scales more favourably, staying close to the
boosted-tree baselines at full training size.

\subsection{Interpretability Comparison}

Table~\ref{tab:xrfm-interpretability} lists the top-5 AGOP-ranked features from each trained xRFM
model. The diagonal highlights physically and behaviourally meaningful variables across all datasets.
Table~\ref{tab:diamonds-feature-importance} then compares AGOP against PCA, mutual information, and
permutation importance on Diamonds: all four methods agree that physical-size variables
(\texttt{x}, \texttt{y}, \texttt{z}, \texttt{carat}) are dominant; permutation importance additionally
weights categorical quality features (\texttt{color\_I}).
AGOP recovers these domain-meaningful rankings despite xRFM's poor predictive performance on Diamonds,
confirming that interpretability and accuracy are not equivalent
(see also Figure~\ref{fig:diamonds-feature-importance-comparison} in Appendix~\ref{app:figures}).
