\section{Results}

This section presents the predictive performance, computational efficiency, scalability, and interpretability comparison between xRFM and three tabular learning baselines: XGBoost, LightGBM, and MLP. All results are generated through the unified experimental pipeline described in Section~\ref{sec:xrfm-config} and the baseline configuration sections, using the same train/validation/test split and evaluation metrics.

\paragraph{Regression tasks.}
For Diamonds and Superconductivity, tree-based baselines dominate. LightGBM achieves the lowest RMSE on both regression datasets, consistent with previous evidence that boosted trees are strong default models for tabular prediction~\citep{grinsztajn2022tree,holzmuller2024better}. xRFM is close to the boosted-tree baselines on Superconductivity, where its RMSE is 9.454 compared with 9.302 for XGBoost and 9.209 for LightGBM. However, it underperforms substantially on Diamonds, where its RMSE of 1496.72 is much higher than LightGBM's 535.99. This suggests that the AGOP-driven representation used by xRFM is not automatically sufficient for large mixed-type regression tasks without further tuning.

\paragraph{Binary classification tasks.}
For Online Shoppers and Stroke, XGBoost and LightGBM achieve the strongest AUC-ROC, showing robust ranking performance under class imbalance. xRFM is competitive but not best on these two tasks, reaching AUC values of 0.9018 and 0.7605 respectively. Its most favourable result is HR Attrition, the smallest dataset in the benchmark. On this dataset, xRFM achieves the highest accuracy (0.8673), although LightGBM has the highest AUC (0.7886). This pattern suggests that xRFM can be useful in small-sample settings where its feature-learning mechanism may identify stable directions, but AUC-based ranking performance still favours tree ensembles in several cases.

\paragraph{Overall performance pattern.}
The main performance conclusion is that XGBoost and LightGBM remain stronger general-purpose models for tabular prediction, while xRFM has selective advantages rather than universal dominance. This is aligned with recent tabular benchmarking work showing that deep or feature-learning approaches can be competitive but require careful adaptation to match boosted-tree baselines~\citep{grinsztajn2022tree,holzmuller2024better,hollmann2025tabpfn}.

\paragraph{Scalability observations.}
The Diamonds subsampling experiment exposes a severe scalability and accuracy limitation for xRFM. Its training time increases from 0.77s at $n=1{,}000$ to 226.44s at $n=30{,}000$, while RMSE only improves from 1657.0 to 1487.5. In contrast, LightGBM reaches 535.3 RMSE at $n=30{,}000$ while training in less than one second. This indicates that xRFM's gradient-based feature-learning step can become expensive on large mixed-type regression data.

Superconductivity gives a more favourable view of xRFM. As $n_\text{train}$ increases from 1{,}000 to 12{,}757, its RMSE improves from 14.07 to 9.46, approaching XGBoost and LightGBM. Its training time also scales moderately from 0.08s to 1.56s. The contrast between Diamonds and Superconductivity suggests that xRFM may be more competitive when the data is fully numerical and high-dimensional, while it struggles more on mixed categorical/numerical datasets with strong threshold-like patterns.

\subsection{Interpretability Comparison}

The interpretability advantage of xRFM comes from its AGOP-derived feature importance. In our implementation, the feature-importance extraction routine first attempts to read the learned AGOP matrix or diagonal values from the internal xRFM model and then ranks feature names by the resulting values. Table~\ref{tab:xrfm-interpretability} summarises the top-five xRFM features for selected datasets.

\begin{table}[H]
  \centering
  \caption{xRFM AGOP-diagonal feature importance extracted from the trained models.}
  \label{tab:xrfm-interpretability}
  \begin{tabular}{ll}
    \toprule
    Dataset & Top AGOP-ranked features \\
    \midrule
    Diamonds & \texttt{num\_\_y}, \texttt{num\_\_x}, \texttt{num\_\_carat}, \texttt{num\_\_z}, \texttt{num\_\_depth} \\
    Superconductivity & \texttt{num\_\_number\_of\_elements}, \texttt{num\_\_mean\_atomic\_mass}, \texttt{num\_\_wtd\_mean\_atomic\_mass}, \texttt{num\_\_gmean\_atomic\_mass}, \texttt{num\_\_wtd\_gmean\_atomic\_mass} \\
    Online Shoppers & \texttt{num\_\_PageValues}, \texttt{num\_\_ExitRates}, \texttt{num\_\_ProductRelated}, \texttt{num\_\_ProductRelated\_Duration}, \texttt{num\_\_BounceRates} \\
    Stroke & \texttt{num\_\_avg\_glucose\_level}, \texttt{num\_\_age}, \texttt{num\_\_bmi}, \texttt{cat\_\_work\_type\_Self-employed}, \texttt{cat\_\_smoking\_status\_smokes} \\
    HR Attrition & \texttt{cat\_\_OverTime\_No}, \texttt{cat\_\_OverTime\_Yes}, \texttt{cat\_\_BusinessTravel\_Travel\_Frequently}, \texttt{num\_\_TotalWorkingYears}, \texttt{cat\_\_BusinessTravel\_Non-Travel} \\
    \bottomrule
  \end{tabular}
\end{table}

The AGOP rankings are broadly interpretable. For Diamonds, xRFM focuses on physical size variables such as $x$, $y$, $z$, and carat, which are plausible drivers of price. For Online Shoppers, \texttt{PageValues} is ranked highest, which is consistent with the idea that pages with higher commercial value are strongly related to purchase intention. For Stroke, glucose level, age, and BMI are prioritised, matching common risk factors used in health-related predictive modelling. For HR Attrition, overtime and business-travel variables dominate, suggesting that xRFM identifies work-intensity indicators as major contributors to attrition predictions.

Table~\ref{tab:interpretability-methods} compares AGOP diagonal importance with PCA, mutual information, and permutation importance at the method level. We do not treat these explanations as interchangeable: each method answers a different question about the model or data. AGOP asks which features the learned xRFM function is locally sensitive to; PCA asks which directions explain input variance; MI asks which individual features have statistical dependence with the target; and permutation importance asks how much test performance drops when a feature is corrupted.

\begin{table}[H]
  \centering
  \caption{Conceptual comparison of interpretability methods used for tabular features.}
  \label{tab:interpretability-methods}
  \begin{tabular}{llll}
    \toprule
    Method & Supervised? & Model-specific? & Main interpretation \\
    \midrule
    AGOP diagonal & Yes & Yes & Sensitivity of xRFM prediction to each feature \\
    PCA loadings & No & No & Directions explaining input variance \\
    Mutual information & Yes & No & Univariate statistical dependence with target \\
    Permutation importance & Yes & Model-agnostic & Test-performance drop after feature shuffle \\
    \bottomrule
  \end{tabular}
\end{table}

Compared with PCA, AGOP is more directly connected to prediction because it is computed from gradients of the learned function rather than from input covariance alone. Compared with MI, AGOP can reflect the behaviour of a multivariate model, whereas MI is typically calculated feature by feature. Compared with permutation importance, AGOP is cheaper to extract after model training, but permutation importance has a clearer link to performance degradation. Therefore, the AGOP diagonal is best interpreted as an internal explanation of xRFM's learned feature geometry rather than a universal ground-truth ranking of feature importance.
