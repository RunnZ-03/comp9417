\section{Results}

This section compares xRFM with XGBoost, LightGBM, and MLP in terms of predictive performance, computational efficiency, scalability, and interpretability. All models are evaluated using the same preprocessing pipeline, train-validation-test split, and evaluation metrics described in Section~2.

\paragraph{Regression tasks.}
LightGBM achieves the lowest RMSE on both regression datasets. xRFM is competitive on Superconductivity, where its RMSE of 9.454 is close to LightGBM's 9.209 and XGBoost's 9.302. However, xRFM performs much worse on Diamonds, with RMSE 1496.72 compared with 535.99 for LightGBM and 544.68 for XGBoost. This suggests that AGOP-based feature learning does not automatically outperform tree ensembles on large mixed-type tabular regression.

\paragraph{Classification tasks.}
For Online Shoppers and Stroke, XGBoost and LightGBM obtain the strongest AUC-ROC scores. xRFM remains reasonably competitive on Online Shoppers, but its AUC is below the boosted-tree models. xRFM's strongest result is on HR Attrition, where it achieves the highest accuracy of 0.8673, suggesting a modest advantage in small-sample classification. MLP obtains high accuracy on Stroke but very low AUC, showing that accuracy alone can be misleading under severe class imbalance.

On Diamonds, xRFM shows a clear scalability limitation. Its training time increases from 0.77s to 226.44s as $n_\text{train}$ grows from 1{,}000 to 30{,}000, while RMSE only improves from 1657.0 to 1487.5. In contrast, LightGBM reaches 535.3 RMSE at 30{,}000 training samples while training in less than one second. On Superconductivity, xRFM scales more favourably: its RMSE improves from 14.07 to 9.46 and remains close to XGBoost and LightGBM at full size. This contrast suggests that xRFM is better suited to fully numerical, high-dimensional regression than to large mixed-type datasets with strong threshold and categorical effects.

The empirical comparison partly supports the theoretical argument made in Section~2.1. AGOP is supervised and model-specific, so it should identify features that the learned xRFM function is sensitive to. On Diamonds, AGOP ranks \texttt{y}, \texttt{x}, \texttt{carat}, and \texttt{z} among the most important features, which agrees with mutual information and permutation importance that physical size and carat weight are central predictors of diamond price. PCA also ranks \texttt{x}, \texttt{y}, \texttt{z}, and \texttt{carat} highly, but this is expected because these variables dominate input variance rather than necessarily target relevance.

The main divergence is that permutation importance assigns additional importance to categorical quality features such as \texttt{color\_I}, while AGOP focuses more heavily on continuous size-related coordinates. This difference is informative: AGOP reflects the sensitivity structure of the fitted xRFM model, whereas permutation importance directly measures test-performance degradation after feature corruption. A surprising finding is that AGOP produces intuitively meaningful feature rankings even though xRFM performs poorly on Diamonds compared with XGBoost and LightGBM. This suggests that interpretability and predictive accuracy are not equivalent. AGOP can identify sensible prediction-relevant directions, but the xRFM prediction pipeline may still be less effective than boosted trees at modelling sharp axis-aligned thresholds and categorical interactions in this dataset.
