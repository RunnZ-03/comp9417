\section{Supplementary Experiments and Figures}
\label{app:supplementary}

This appendix provides supplementary material for the main empirical analysis. It includes an additional AGOP-based splitting verification and supplementary figures for AGOP feature importance, Superconductivity scalability, and performance-efficiency trade-offs. These materials support the main findings in Sections~3 and~4 but are not required for understanding the core report.

\subsection{AGOP-Based Splitting Criterion: Scratch Implementation}
\label{app:agop-split}

The xRFM algorithm selects split directions using the Average Gradient Outer Product (AGOP). In the original xRFM formulation, the AGOP provides a supervised direction in input space that is relevant to prediction~\citep{beaglehole2025xrfm}. To better understand this mechanism, we implemented a simplified AGOP-based splitting criterion from scratch using ridge regression.

For a linear prediction model
\[
f(x) = w^\top x + b,
\]
the gradient is constant:
\[
\nabla f(x) = w.
\]
Therefore, the AGOP reduces to
\[
G_f = \mathbb{E}_x\left[\nabla f(x)\nabla f(x)^\top\right]
    = ww^\top.
\]
The diagonal entries are
\[
[G_f]_{ii}=w_i^2,
\]
which provide coordinate-wise feature importance scores. The leading eigenvector of $G_f$ is proportional to $w$, so the split direction is
\[
v_1 = \frac{w}{\|w\|}.
\]

In our scratch implementation, the threshold $t^*$ is selected by scanning candidate quantiles of the projection $v_1^\top X$ and choosing the threshold that minimises the weighted target variance after splitting. This simplified implementation is not intended to replace the official xRFM library. Instead, it verifies the intuition that AGOP-based directions can recover prediction-relevant coordinates.

\paragraph{Synthetic verification.}
We construct a synthetic dataset with $n=300$ and $d=10$, where the target is generated as
\[
y = 3x_0 - 2x_1 + \varepsilon.
\]
Thus, the true relevant features are $x_0$ and $x_1$, while $x_2$ to $x_9$ are noise variables. Table~\ref{tab:appendix-agop-synthetic} compares the scratch AGOP diagonal with a numerical AGOP estimate obtained from the fitted xRFM predictor.

\begin{table}[H]
  \centering
  \caption{AGOP diagonal on the synthetic dataset. Noise features $x_2$--$x_9$ receive low scores under both methods.}
  \label{tab:appendix-agop-synthetic}
  \small
  \begin{tabular}{lcc}
    \toprule
    Feature & Scratch $w_i^2$ & xRFM numerical AGOP \\
    \midrule
    $x_0$ (coefficient $+3$) & 9.23 & 13.03 \\
    $x_1$ (coefficient $-2$) & 3.93 & 5.28 \\
    $x_2$--$x_9$ (noise) & $\leq 0.03$ & $\leq 0.30$ \\
    \midrule
    Top-2 identified & $\{x_0,x_1\}$ & $\{x_0,x_1\}$ \\
    Cosine similarity & \multicolumn{2}{c}{\textbf{0.9985}} \\
    \bottomrule
  \end{tabular}
\end{table}

Both methods correctly identify $x_0$ and $x_1$ as the top two features. The high cosine similarity indicates that the scratch implementation recovers nearly the same feature direction as the numerical AGOP estimate. This supports the interpretation of the AGOP diagonal as a coordinate-wise feature relevance measure.

\subsection{Additional AGOP Feature Importance Visualisation}
\label{app:feature-importance-bars}

Figure~\ref{fig:appendix-feature-importance} visualises the top-five AGOP diagonal features for Diamonds and Superconductivity. For Diamonds, AGOP emphasises physical size and weight-related variables such as \texttt{y}, \texttt{x}, \texttt{carat}, \texttt{z}, and \texttt{depth}. For Superconductivity, the top AGOP features are related to the number of elements and atomic-mass statistics. These rankings are consistent with the interpretation that AGOP highlights coordinates to which the learned xRFM prediction function is most sensitive.

\subsection{Supplementary Superconductivity Scalability Analysis}
\label{app:superconductivity-scaling}

The main Results section includes the Diamonds scalability curve because Diamonds is the largest dataset in our experiments and directly satisfies the large-dataset subsampling requirement. Figure~\ref{fig:appendix-super-scaling} adds the corresponding Superconductivity scalability curve. The Superconductivity results show that xRFM's RMSE decreases steadily as the training size grows and remains close to XGBoost and LightGBM at full training size, while its training time remains far lower than MLP.

\subsection{Training-Time and Accuracy Trade-off}
\label{app:time-accuracy-tradeoff}

Figure~\ref{fig:appendix-time-accuracy} shows the trade-off between training time and predictive error. A better model should ideally appear closer to the lower-left region, indicating both low RMSE and low training time. On Diamonds, LightGBM and XGBoost dominate the trade-off, while xRFM is both slower and less accurate. On Superconductivity, xRFM is closer to the boosted-tree baselines, suggesting that its feature-learning mechanism may be more suitable for fully numerical high-dimensional regression than for large mixed-type regression.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.95\textwidth]{figures/time_accuracy_tradeoff.pdf}
    \caption{Training-time and predictive-performance trade-off for Diamonds and Superconductivity. Lower RMSE and lower training time indicate a better efficiency-performance trade-off.}
    \label{fig:appendix-time-accuracy}
\end{figure}

\paragraph{Appendix summary.}
The supplementary results reinforce the main conclusions of the report. AGOP provides interpretable feature rankings that are often aligned with domain intuition, especially for numerical predictors. However, meaningful AGOP feature rankings do not guarantee superior predictive performance. The supplementary scalability and trade-off plots show that xRFM is competitive on Superconductivity but struggles on Diamonds, where boosted-tree methods achieve a better accuracy-efficiency trade-off.
