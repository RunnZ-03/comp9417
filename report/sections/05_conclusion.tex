\section{Conclusion}
In this project, we replicated xRFM — a novel average gradient outer product (AGOP)-driven feature learning method for tabular data and conducted a rigorous, reproducible comparative evaluation against three standard tabular learning baselines (XGBoost, LightGBM, MLP) across five public datasets covering regression and binary classification tasks. We built a unified end-to-end experimental pipeline to ensure consistent preprocessing, training, and evaluation, eliminating cross-model discrepancies in data usage and metric calculation.

Our key experimental findings are threefold. First, tree-based gradient boosting models (LightGBM, XGBoost) remain robust, high-performance baselines for general tabular tasks: LightGBM achieved top predictive performance on 4 of 5 datasets, with far better training efficiency and scalability than MLP across all subsample sizes. Second, xRFM showed competitive performance on small-scale, low-dimensional classification (achieving the highest accuracy on the HR Attrition dataset), highlighting the strength of its AGOP feature learning in small-sample settings, but underperformed on large-scale high-dimensional regression, requiring further hyperparameter tuning. Third, standard MLP architectures have notable limitations for tabular data, particularly on highly imbalanced classification tasks and large-scale data with poor training time scaling.

For future work, we identify three key extensions: targeted hyperparameter optimisation for xRFM to improve its large-scale performance, a full interpretability comparison between xRFM's AGOP feature importance and standard methods, and validation of xRFM's generalisability on more diverse tabular tasks including multi-class classification and high-dimensional sparse data.
